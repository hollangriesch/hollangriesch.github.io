Did the Big Ten underachieve in this years NCAA tournament?

b1g
basketball
analysis
Author

Hollan Griesch

Published

April 15, 2023

March used to be the month when Big Ten teams shined. From 2000-2019 the Big Ten placed 16 teams in the Final Four, the most of any conference during that time.

This success has started to shift with only one of the eight Big Ten teams in the tournament making it to the Sweet Sixteen this past season. The Big Ten also had a historically bad showing in the 2021-22 NCAA tournament when each of their five teams seeded eight or higher performed below expectations.

The question is what is causing this? Are Big Ten teams really playing that much worse in the postseason compared to the regular season? Or is it just some bad luck?

library(tidyverse)
library(gt)
library(ggalt)
library(ggtext)
mbb_team_box <- hoopR::load_mbb_team_box(2023)

regularseason23 <- mbb_team_box %>% filter(game_date > "2022-11-07" & game_date <= "2023-03-12") 

big10 <- c("Nebraska", "Iowa", "Indiana", "Illinois", "Maryland", "Rutgers", "Michigan", "Michigan St", "Ohio State", "Penn State", "Northwestern", "Purdue", "Minnesota", "Wisconsin")
bigregular <- regularseason23 %>%
  filter(team_short_display_name %in% big10)

tournament <- c("Maryland", "Illinois", "Indiana", "Iowa", "Michigan St", "Northwestern", "Penn State", "Purdue")

postseason23 <- mbb_team_box %>% filter(game_date > "2023-03-14" & game_date <= "2023-04-03")

bigpost <- postseason23 %>%
  filter(team_short_display_name %in% tournament)

regularthreepct <- bigregular %>% 
  group_by(team_short_display_name) %>% 
  summarise(
    count = n(),
    SeasonThreePct = sum(three_point_field_goal_pct),
    TotalGames = n()
  ) %>%
  mutate(
    ThreePointPct = SeasonThreePct/TotalGames
  )

ggplot() +
  geom_bar(data = regularthreepct, aes(x = reorder(team_short_display_name, ThreePointPct), weight = ThreePointPct)) +
  coord_flip() +
   labs(
    x="School", 
    y="Three Point Percentage", 
    title="How did Big Ten teams shoot the three in the regular season?", 
    subtitle="Michigan State led the conference in regular season three point percentage", 
    caption="Source: hoopR | By Hollan Griesch"
    )   +             
  theme_minimal() +
  theme(
    title = element_text(color = "black", size = 10, face = "bold"),
    plot.subtitle = element_text(size = 8),
    plot.background = element_rect(fill = "white"),
    axis.text = element_text(size = 8, color = "red"),

  )

In the regular season, no Big Ten teams shot under 30% from beyond the arc.

However, in the NCAA tournament four of the conference’s eight teams shot below that mark. In fact, Penn State was the only team that actually improved their three point shooting from the regular to post season.

Obviously the biggest story of this year’s tournament was the upset of one-seeded Purdue by 16-seeded Fairleigh Dickson. Let’s take a look at what went wrong with the Boilermakers during March Madness.

Code 
bigmbb <- bigregular %>%
  select(team_short_display_name, field_goals_attempted, field_goals_made, field_goal_pct) %>%
  mutate_at(vars(-team_short_display_name), as.numeric) %>%
  group_by(team_short_display_name) %>%
  summarize(
    TotalFG = sum(field_goals_made),
    TotalFGA = sum(field_goals_attempted)
  ) %>%
  mutate(FGPct = TotalFG/TotalFGA) %>%
  rename(Team = team_short_display_name) %>%
  select(Team, FGPct) %>%
  arrange(desc(FGPct))

When the postseason rolled around Purdue’s field goal percentage dropped by a whopping 10%. The decrease in their three point percentage combined with this decline in overall field goal percentage proved to be a recipe for disaster for the Boilermakers.

The struggles offensively were visible all across the board for the Big Ten as six of the eight tournament teams saw a smaller percentage of their shots fall then they did during the regular season.

Lets see if the teams were able to make up for slow offense with their defense.

Code 
turnovers <- bigpost %>%
  group_by(team_short_display_name) %>%
  summarise (
    Giveaways = sum(total_turnovers),
    Takeaways = sum(steals)) %>%
rename(Team = team_short_display_name)

ggplot() + 
  geom_dumbbell(
    data=turnovers, 
    aes(y=Team, x=Takeaways, xend=Giveaways),
    size = 2,
    colour = "grey",
    colour_x = "green",
    colour_xend = "red") +
  labs(
    title="Did Big Ten team's win the turnover battle in the postseason?", 
    subtitle = "No Big Ten team had more steals than turnovers in the tournament",
    caption="Source: hoopR | By Hollan Griesch"
  ) + 
  theme_minimal() +
  theme(
    title = element_text(color = "black", size = 14, face = "bold"),
    plot.subtitle = element_text(size = 11),
    axis.text = element_text(size =8, color = "black"),
    plot.title.position = "plot"
  )

The Big Ten team’s were not able to win the turnover battle in the NCAA tournament which coupled with struggling offense resulted in another disappointing postseason for the conference.

It is clear that the Big Ten has been underperforming in March Madness the past few seasons and that begs the question is it just bad luck? Based on the fact that Big Ten teams have had worse performances in nearly every category in the postseason compared to the regular season there has to be more to blame than just bad luck.

It will be interesting to see if the conference can turn this underachievement around and get back to their winning ways since the conference hasn’t produced a national champion since Michigan State back in 2000.
